  1. Fusion of two BioBricks by 3A Assembly cloning

    2. Aim

    To establish BioBrick cloning at our high school.


    Materials

    The 3A Assembly kit (iGEM).
    Competent E. coli DH5α cells (kindly provided by Vanderbilt University, TN).


    Methods

    Four steps were performed:

    1. Digestion of two BioBrick plasmid DNAs to liberate their two DNA insert parts: a promoter-ribosome binding site (RBS) part and a monomeric red fluorescent protein (RFP)-coding DNA part.
    2. Ligation of these two insert parts into a linearized plasmid backbone.
    3. Transformation of E. coli competent cells, and
    4. Overnight growth of transformed cells on antibiotic-agar plates.

      5. Detailed steps can be found in official publishings of iGEM.

    Results

    This cloning experiment produced 3 plates each with hundreds of red colonies as expected (see photograph with red colonies).

    Right: the transformation control plate showed that our competent cells transformed efficiently when using a plasmid expressing RFP.
    Middle: the ligation control plate showed that our DNA cut with restriction enzymes religated efficiently to express RFP.
    Left: our test plate also expressed RFP, demonstrating that we successfully fused the promoter-RBS part upstream of the RFP-coding part in the destination vector.


    Conclusion

    We successfully established BioBrick cloning for the first time at our high school.


  2. Towards engineering production of therapeutic sage terpenoids in E. coli and yeast

    3. Aim

    To clone the gene for sage bornyl diphosphate synthase by PCR followed by plasmid cloning in E. coli.


    Materials

    Sage plant genomic DNA (kindly provided by Vanderbilt University, TN).
    PCR reagents (kindly provided by Vanderbilt University, TN).
    Synthetic oligodeoxyribonucleotide primers (we ordered them from Life Technologies).
    Agarose gel electrophoresis equipment.
    pSB1C3 BioBrick plasmid vector and cloning enzymes (iGEM).
    Competent E. coli DH5α cells (kindly provided by Vanderbilt University, TN).


    Methods

    The coding sequence for sage bornyl diphosphate synthase has been determined (1801 nucleotides; see Overview).

    However, the size of the gene is presently unknown and is probably more than twice as big as the coding sequence due the high probability of the presence of introns. We proposed to amplify the whole gene by PCR, then clone it into a BioBrick vector in E. coli for ultimate protein over-expression in yeast. Expression in yeast instead of E. coli would be necessary because, in contrast to bacteria, yeast contains intron-splicing machinery.

    Using a computer program (CLC Main Workbench) we designed primers flanking the coding region (see Overview), each containing an overhang with a BioBrick restriction site. We then performed PCR with Taq polymerase according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Reaction products were separated by agarose gel electrophoresis, excised from the gel and used in the BioBrick cloning method described in section 1 above.


    Results

    Analysis of two different PCR reactions by electrophoresis on a 0.8% agarose gel and staining with ethidium bromide did not reveal prominent, gene-sized, gel bands. Nevertheless, we excised the region of the gel in the 2-6 kb size range (Figure S-5), extracted the DNA, re-PCR amplified it, cut it with BioBrick enzymes and attempted to clone it in the pSB1C3 vector. Unfortunately, no antibiotic-resistant colonies were observed.


    Conclusion

    Our inability to produce visible PCR products from the sage bornyl diphosphate synthase gene was most likely due to the large size of the gene, though we cannot rule out other possibilities such as sub-optimal primer design or inefficient PCR reaction conditions. Our proposed trouble-shooting plan is firstly to perform control PCR reactions to check our protocol. Then we would aim to prepare shorter PCR products of known length by reverse-transcribing the bornyl diphosphate synthase mRNA into cDNA using the same downstream primer, followed by PCR using the same primer pair. This approach also has the potential advantage of enabling protein over-expression in either E. coli or yeast because cDNA does not contain introns.
